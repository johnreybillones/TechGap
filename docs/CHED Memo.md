This document, **CHED Memorandum Order (CMO) No. 25, Series of 2015**, establishes the revised policies, standards, and guidelines (PSGs) for Bachelor of Science in Computer Science (BSCS), Information Systems (BSIS), and Information Technology (BSIT) programs in the Philippines.

Below is a summary of the content and the specific constraints you'll need to program into your curriculum revision AI.

---

## **Executive Summary of CMO No. 25 s. 2015**

The primary shift in this order is the move toward **Outcomes-Based Education (OBE)**, focusing on learner competencies rather than just course completion.

* **Program Specifications:** Defines the distinct nature of BSCS (theory and algorithms), BSIS (business process integration), and BSIT (infrastructure and solutions).  
* **Minimum Standards:** Prescribes the required "graduate outcomes" that every student must possess regardless of the institution.  
* **Curriculum Structure:** Mandates a minimum of **146 units** (for non-COEs), including General Education, Common, Professional, and Elective courses.  
* **Resource Requirements:** Sets strict rules for faculty qualifications (e.g., Master’s degrees), library holdings, and laboratory ratios.

---

## **CHED’s Role in Your University’s Curriculum**

CHED acts as the **regulatory body** that sets the "floor" for quality. Their role affects your curriculum in three ways:

1. **Authority to Operate:** No HEI (Higher Education Institution) can offer these degrees without securing authority from CHED by proving they meet these PSGs.  
2. **Minimum Standard Enforcement:** CHED prescribes the "core" that cannot be removed. However, they provide "ample space for HEIs to innovate" and add courses suited to their specific mission.  
3. **Standardization of Competencies:** They ensure that a BSCS graduate from one school has the same fundamental "Knowledge for Solving Computing Problems" as a graduate from another.

---

## **Constraints for Your AI Decision Support System**

When your AI suggests curriculum revisions, it must operate within these "hard" constraints to ensure the resulting curriculum remains compliant with Philippine law:

### **1\. The 146-Unit Minimum**

Unless your university is a Center of Excellence (COE), the curriculum must total at least **146 units**.

* **General Education:** 54 units.  
* **Common Courses:** 18 units.  
* **Professional Courses:** 48 units.  
* **PE/NSTP:** 14 units.

### **2\. Mandatory Core Computing Courses**

Your AI cannot remove or significantly dilute these six core courses (18 units total) required for all three programs:

* Introduction to Computing (CC101)  
* Computer Programming 1 & 2 (CC102, CC103)  
* Data Structures and Algorithms (CC104)  
* Information Management (CC105)  
* Applications Development and Emerging Technologies (CC106)

### **3\. Program-Specific Terminal Requirements**

The AI must distinguish between a **Thesis** and a **Capstone Project**:

* **BSCS:** Requires a **Thesis** focused on computing theory or scientific work.  
* **BSIS/BSIT:** Requires a **Capstone Project** focused on infrastructure or business applications.

### **4\. Internship Hours**

There are specific minimum hour requirements for the practicum component:

* **BSCS:** Minimum 162 hours.  
* **BSIS & BSIT:** Minimum 486 hours.

### **5\. Curriculum Mapping & Outcomes**

Any new course suggested by the AI must be "mapped" to the prescribed **Program Outcomes**. For example, a BSCS revision must prove that its courses collectively satisfy outcomes CS01 through CS10 (covering everything from "Problem Analysis" to "Life-Long Learning").

### **6\. Faculty Qualification Constraints**

If the AI suggests a new specialization (e.g., Intelligent Systems), it should flag that **60% of professional courses** must be taught by degree holders in that specific program, and **30% of full-time faculty** must hold graduate degrees in the discipline.
